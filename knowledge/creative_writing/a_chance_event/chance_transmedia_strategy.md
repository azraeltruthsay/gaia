# Chance / Fidget — Transmedia Strategy

*This document contains the publication architecture. Protect it.*

---

## Terminology

- The project is titled **A Chance Event**
- Each installment — whether prose or comic — is called an **Event**
- Events are not numbered episodes. They are named. The name is the thing.
- Example Event names: The Collision, The Coin, The Catch, The Turning Point, The Diner

---

## Core Philosophy

This is not multimedia adaptation. The novella does not adapt the comic. The comic does not adapt the novella. They are **adjacent windows into the same living universe** — each complete on its own, each revealing something the other cannot, and together producing an experience neither can provide alone.

The audience is not meant to consume both to get the "full" story. They are meant to consume both to get a **different** story than either tells alone.

---

## The Tagline

*"The timeline didn't repeat, but it did rhyme."*

This applies to the diner scene divergence, to the relationship between the two timelines, and to the entire project. Put it on the cover of something.

---

## Phase One: Establishment — Timeline One (Fidget's Story)

Timeline One opens with **Chance's birth** — prose, novella form. The particle accelerator. The collision. The scientists asking the wrong question.

From there, Timeline One alternates between novella and comic, each installment covering scenes the other medium never revisits. No redundancy. Gidget appears in every scene of Timeline One, but he is never the focus — a background detail, a figure in the corner, a cat sleeping somewhere he shouldn't be. **Where's Waldo with cosmic implications.** Readers who go back after finishing Timeline Two will find him everywhere they didn't think to look.

Timeline One ends with the diner scene: **coin lands heads up.** Chance smiles at the rain. Gidget's presence is ambiguous, possibly unmentioned.

---

## Phase Two: Establishment — Timeline Two (Gidget's Story)

Timeline Two opens with **Gidget's birth** — prose, matching the form of Timeline One's opening. Nine kittens collapsing into one. The same facility. The same moment. The vet asking the wrong questions.

From there, Timeline Two follows an alternating media structure that weaves **Fidget arc installments** with **Gidget mini-story installments**, always flipping medium from the previous Gidget entry:

- Prose Gidget story → Comic Fidget arc → Comic Gidget story → Prose Fidget arc → and so on

The Fidget arcs in Timeline Two are the alternate-medium versions of Timeline One's scenes — covering the same *period* of Chance's life but through different scenes, different angles, different moments. Gidget is not background here. He is making decisions. Small ones, mostly. Cat-sized edits to reality that accumulate.

Timeline Two ends with the diner scene: **coin lands tails up.** Gidget bats it off the table. Chance sighs, retrieves it, and is caught off guard by Serenity appearing with his coffee. After he smiles at the rain, we hold on Gidget — sitting where the coin was, looking directly at the reader. No caption. No dialogue.

---

## The Reverse-Initiation

Gidget's coin bat at the end of Timeline Two is not just a cat being a cat.

It is the **save state creation** — the initiation point of Timeline Two's entire existence, operating in reverse. Set from the endpoint, radiating backward through the whole timeline. Every subtle difference between Timeline One and Timeline Two is a cat's edit. Gidget reviewed Timeline One from inside it, as a background character with full awareness, and decided some adjustments were warranted.

Not dramatic ones. Just rhyming ones.

Timeline Two was always Gidget's story. The diner scene is where everyone finds out.

---

## Event Naming

Events are named, not numbered. The name is the thing — it should carry the weight of what the moment actually is.

**Not every Event name needs to be a coin pun.** The most pivotal Events, where the story takes its most significant turns, earn coin resonance naturally. Other Events stand on their own with their own weight. The rule is: the name earns the pun, the pun doesn't earn the name.

Current named Events:
- **The Collision** — Chance's birth (impact, the moment things smash together)
- **The Coin** — the first flip (also: to coin something, to create it from nothing)
- **The Catch** — the speedster intercepts the coin (also: what closes a save state)
- **The Turning Point** — the battle scene (also: the rotational axis of a spinning coin)
- **The Diner** — Timeline One's final shared Event
- **The Diner on the Corner** — Timeline Two's final shared Event

The coin puns are not announced. They are discovered.

---

## The Gravitational Field Principle

No Event should be written as definitively final — too closed at the beginning, too resolved at the end. Each Event exists within a **gravitational field of adjacent Events** that could be excavated later without violating the non-redundancy rule.

The mechanism: when fans want to *see* a prose Event rendered visually, the answer is never to adapt that Event into a comic. The answer is to publish a genuinely new Event set in the same timeframe — immediately before, immediately after, or alongside — that couldn't exist without the original.

**Example:** After The Diner exists in prose, a reader wants to see the coin sitting flat on the table as a visual. Instead of adapting The Diner into a comic, publish a new visual Event set twenty minutes later on the same street. Serenity's shift ending. The corner at dusk. The fish market closing up. The emotional world of The Diner, continued — not repeated.

The fan gets their visual. The prose version remains sovereign. The non-redundancy rule holds. The universe expands.

This works in every direction:
- A scene set immediately before an Event, from a different perspective
- A scene set immediately after, following a different character
- A simultaneous scene happening elsewhere in the same world during the same moment
- A scene that could only exist because the original Event established its context

The universe is **infinitely expandable without ever becoming redundant.** You are not revisiting Events. You are excavating the space around them.

Every Event that gets published creates gravitational pull. New Events orbit it without colliding with it.

---

## The Split — Where Timelines Diverge

**The Diner / The Diner on the Corner is the last shared canonical moment.**

Both timelines render this Event. Neither renders it identically — details differ in ways that seem like artistic license until they don't. The geometry mirrors. The coin lands differently. The cat is on his table.

After this Event, the two timelines **diverge completely.**

Events in Timeline One's post-Diner world do not occur in Timeline Two's post-Diner world. Both are internally consistent. Both are real. They are parallel branches from the moment Chance set the coin down flat and stopped controlling outcomes.

The narrative architecture *becomes* the story's theme at the moment of publication.

---

## The Non-Redundancy Rule

Within each timeline, the two media run **parallel but non-overlapping**. No scene appears in both the novella and comic installments of the same timeline. Events may be referenced across media but are never re-shown. The reader builds a complete picture from non-redundant angles.

This rule is the spine of the entire architecture. The Gravitational Field Principle expands the universe *around* Events without ever violating it.

---

## Phase Three: Remastering

Once both timelines are established:

Past events from each timeline are **remastered into the opposite medium**.

The novella's early scenes are rendered as comics. The comic's early scenes are written as prose.

These are not simply reformats. Each remaster contains **a minor, specific difference** from the original scene — a detail changed, a line of dialogue shifted, a presence or absence that shouldn't be possible if both timelines share the same origin.

True readers will find them. They will not be announced.

---

## The Fan Experience

**Casual reader:** Encounters one medium, gets a complete, satisfying story.

**Engaged reader:** Encounters both media, notices the parallel structure, appreciates the architecture.

**True fan:** Begins cross-referencing. Finds the diner scene rendered twice and notices it isn't quite the same. Begins hunting backward. Finds the divergence point — or thinks they do. Argues about it. Builds spreadsheets.

The question "which timeline is the real one?" has the same answer for readers as it does for Chance:

**Neither. And both. You're in the one you chose to follow.**

---

## Structural Notes

- The diner scene should be the **last thing published in Phase One**, simultaneously in both media if possible
- The remaster releases in Phase Three should be staggered to maximize discovery
- No official confirmation of the divergence point should ever be given — let the canon remain genuinely ambiguous
- The minor differences in remastered scenes should be subtle enough to require real attention but legible enough that finding them feels earned, not arbitrary

---

## A Note on Intent

This structure would frustrate readers who want everything in one place.

It would delight readers who understand that some things require commitment to the timeline you're in.

Chance would find it cosmically funny. The whole architecture is a coin flip the audience gets to make.
