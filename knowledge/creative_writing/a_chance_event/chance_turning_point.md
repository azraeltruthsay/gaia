# Chance / Fidget — The Turning Point (Battle Scene Seed)

*This is a seed document — the scene is not yet fully written, but the bones are here. This is a pivotal moment in Chance's arc: the transition from reluctant, moody participant to proactive (if quiet) hero.*

---

## Context

This scene occurs after:
- Chance's release from juvenile detention
- His provisional acceptance into the hero community
- A period of moody, reluctant participation — he's there, but not *in* it

He is not yet trusted. Not yet respected. To the other heroes he is an odd, fidgety peripheral figure whose actual power nobody has correctly assessed. They know he does *something* with the coin. They don't know what.

---

## The Setup

A major villain — specifics to be developed — launches an assault that draws the full hero ensemble. It is serious. The kind of event where everyone shows up.

Before the battle, Chance is told, with varying degrees of politeness depending on the hero, to **stand aside and not interfere.** The consensus among the assembled heroes is that this is too important, too dangerous, too precise an operation to have the coin guy wandering around flipping things.

Chance complies.

He finds a position off to the side. Elevated, maybe. Good sightlines.

He starts flipping the coin.

---

## The Battle

The heroes do their thing. The archetypes are visible and legible without requiring deep introduction:

- The high-and-mighty one leads, makes the noble calls
- The grim one works the margins, takes the hits
- The speedster is everywhere at once
- Others fill their roles

Chance watches. Coin flipping. He is keeping save states out of habit — or maybe out of something that isn't quite hope yet.

The battle goes wrong in stages. Not all at once. Each problem is something that *almost* gets handled. The heroes are good. They're just not quite good enough today.

People die. Not background figures — people who have been established enough to matter. Heroes fall. The villain's plan develops a momentum that starts to feel inevitable.

The coin keeps flipping.

---

## The Decision

At some point — the exact beat to be determined — Chance reaches a threshold.

He has been *told* to stay out of it. He has complied. He has watched the timeline he was handed go somewhere he doesn't want to live in.

And then he makes a quiet decision.

Not a dramatic one. No speech to himself. No moment of swelling resolve. He just looks at what's in front of him and decides:

*I'd rather live in a world where they win. Even if they're annoying and overbearing and they told me to sit down.*

He flips the coin one last time. It suspends. Time stops.

---

## The Rollback

Chance speaks to the villain — who is frozen mid-victory, unknowing — before he begins.

**"I'd rather live in a world where you lose. Even if the people who beat you are kind of annoying."**

Then he begins moving through the save states. Not to the beginning — he doesn't need to start over. Just to the specific moments where small things went wrong. He identifies the hinges. The two-second warnings that didn't arrive. The footing that slipped. The structural element that failed. The moment a particular hero was looking the wrong direction.

He fixes details. Nothing grand. Nobody will ever be able to point to what he did and name it heroism because it doesn't look like heroism. It looks like luck.

Each fix is a flip of the suspended coin. We watch the battle reassemble in the margins, quietly, piece by piece.

---

## The Resolution

The heroes win.

Barely — but cleanly. No deaths. The villain's plan collapses at what appears, to everyone present, to be the worst possible moment for him. A cascade of improbable misfortunes at the critical juncture.

The heroes are battered, grateful, and quietly disbelieving at how it came together at the end. They probably congratulate each other. The high-and-mighty one says something about never doubting. The grim one says nothing.

Nobody looks at Chance.

Chance doesn't correct them.

---

## The Villain's Exit

The villain — defeated, confused, furious — looks at the outcome. He runs the numbers in his head. This shouldn't have happened. His plan was sound. His execution was precise. The probability of this outcome was—

**"What... what are the chances..."**

He says it as a genuine question. A broken lament. Not a knowing remark.

Chance, who is the only person in earshot, catches the coin and pockets it.

He doesn't answer.

He walks away.

---

## What This Scene Does

- Establishes the **true scale of Chance's power** for the first time — not through explanation but through demonstration
- Shows that **his compliance was a choice**, not a limitation — and that choosing not to act has real consequences he has to live through
- Gives him a **moral turning point** that isn't about becoming a hero in the traditional sense — it's about deciding he prefers a particular kind of world and being willing to do quiet work to maintain it
- Introduces the villain's **"What are the chances"** line — which lands perfectly because it's genuine, not knowing
- Establishes the dynamic where **Chance never takes credit**, not out of false modesty but because it genuinely doesn't occur to him that credit is something to collect
- The heroes who told him to sit down **never find out what happened.** That's not a setup for a reveal. That's just how it is.

---

## Tonal Notes

This scene should feel heavy before the turn. Real losses. Real weight. The reader should feel the cost of Chance sitting on his hands, and understand why — he was trying to respect their world, their rules, their assessment of him.

The decision to roll back should feel quiet, not triumphant. He's not proving anything to anyone. He's just choosing the timeline he wants to live in.

The villain's final line should land in near-silence. No fanfare. Chance doesn't even pause.

The coin goes in his pocket. He walks into whatever comes next.

## Open Questions

- Who is the villain? What is the plan? The specifics matter for calibrating the stakes.
- Which heroes die in the bad timeline, and does Chance remember them afterward? Does he grieve people who are, from everyone else's perspective, perfectly alive?
- Does anyone notice Chance was flipping the coin throughout? Does anyone ask him afterward what he saw from his vantage point?
- Is there a moment in the fixed timeline where Chance and the villain make brief eye contact — where the villain senses something is wrong with this person specifically, without being able to name why?
